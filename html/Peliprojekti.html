Full Stack Open & Introduction to Python Programming

University of Helsinki

FullStack open is an open university course provided by the computer science department from the university of Helsinki. It has gained global noteriety thanks to its high level of education as well as it has an active comunity of people in their personal discord server.

The introduction to python programming course is a replacement course which I decided was a more impotant course for me to undertake in stead of Full Stack Open. I have some experience in Python programming prior to the course, but its parts are divided into 2 parts, beginner and Advanced. I would take both of these parts, but would go through the beginner part in a faster pace, and mostly do the more demanding exercises.

Description

Full Stack open is divided into 14 parts (0-14) from which parts 0-8 and 13 are written by Matti Luukainen, a professor in the computer science department of University of Helsinki (abbr. HU). The rest of the exercises are created by outside contributors, which include but are not limited to; Terveystalo, Smartly.io and Houston Inc.

The introduction to python programming and the advanced python progamming courses are a part of the Computer science department of the University of Helsinki. Each of the parts are worth 5 ETC's. The course also consists of 14 different parts. If you are able to complete 25% of exercises in a part (number of, varies), you can move on to the next part. Parts 1-4 I decided to do exercises that I deemed fun and challenging, while ignoring things I already knew and could solve in my head.

Some of these include, random, Time and dateTime as well as powerful data processing libraries such as NumPy and Pandas.

Part 7 Consists of:

Conclusion

Python: Beginner

Here are my thought on the beginner course of Python programming:


The course is easy for anyone that that has a little bit of programming experience. As for a total newbie, the course has a great learning curve that is accustomed to higher education students as well as anyone who might be interested in becoming a python programmer. The course opens the door and it's upto the student to use this knowledge for future endeavours. TestMyCode (TMC extention on VS code) is also a forgiving automated code testing environment. Students are quickly taught about the most powerful and versatile IDE there is and they are (kind of) forced into getting used to it. After a while of using VS Code, I can say that, it is still my favourite coding environment.


While there are a lot of positives, there are also some negatives. Most of it comes in the form of not providing almost any information about professionals paths or professional habits. Biggest of these is the lack of debugging methods mentioned. Yes, there is indeed a built-in debugger in (from what I know of) all IDE's on the market. But, there is only a single mention of the word Google in all 7 parts. any decent coder knows that google is the ultimate debugging tool for solving problems and this might be common sense to an experienced coder, but not for someone who's coded for approximately 7 weeks, at the end of the course. Googling is a skill that needs to be taught in order for students to be efficient in it.
